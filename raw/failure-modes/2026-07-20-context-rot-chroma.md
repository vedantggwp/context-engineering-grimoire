## Premise

"Recent developments in LLMs show a trend toward longer context windows, with
the input token count of the latest models reaching the millions. Because these
models achieve near-perfect scores on widely adopted benchmarks like Needle in a
Haystack (NIAH), it's often assumed that their performance is uniform across
long-context tasks."

The report's counter-claim: NIAH "is fundamentally a simple retrieval task"
assessing "direct lexical matching, which may not be representative of flexible,
semantically oriented tasks."

Methodological core: "our experiments hold task complexity constant while varying
only the input length—allowing us to directly measure the effect of input length
alone."

## Stated contributions

- "An evaluation across 18 LLMs, including leading closed-source and open-weights
  models, revealing nonuniform performance with increasing input length."
- "A writeup of observed model-specific behavior patterns when handling
  distractors and varying question-answer similarity."
- The complete replication codebase.

## Experimental scale

For every unique combination of needle type, haystack topic, and haystack
structure, each model was tested across 8 input lengths and 11 needle positions.
Temperature=0 unless incompatible (o3) or discouraged (Qwen thinking mode). Qwen
models extended 32,768 → 131,072 tokens via YaRN. Outputs judged by an aligned
GPT-4.1 judge. Total LLM calls: 194,480, of which 69 were refusals (0.035%).

## Findings as stated in prose

**1. Needle–question similarity.** "As needle-question similarity decreases,
model performance degrades more significantly with increasing input length." At
short input lengths models perform well even on low-similarity pairs. Needle
position was tested across 11 positions with "no notable variation in performance
for this specific NIAH task."

PG-essay needles ranged 0.445–0.775 needle-question similarity; arXiv needles
0.521–0.829; both with <0.1 standard deviation across five embedding models
(text-embedding-3-small, text-embedding-3-large, jina-embeddings-v3,
voyage-3-large, all-MiniLM-L6-v2).

**2. Distractors.** "Even a single distractor reduces performance relative to the
baseline (needle only), and adding four distractors compounds this degradation
further." Impact is non-uniform between distractors. Model-family differences:
"Claude models consistently exhibit the lowest hallucination rates. Specifically,
Claude Sonnet 4 and Opus 4 are particularly conservative and tend to abstain when
uncertain... In contrast, GPT models show the highest rates of hallucination,
often generating confident but incorrect responses when distractors are present."

**3. Needle–haystack similarity.** "Non-uniform effect on model performance." In
the PG-essay haystack, arXiv needles performed significantly better than PG-essay
needles (i.e. models did better when the needle did NOT blend in). In the arXiv
haystack, only minimal difference. The report explicitly declines to generalize:
"Testing across only two topics is insufficient to draw a generalizable
conclusion."

Similarity scores: PG haystack — PG needles 0.529 (var 0.101), arXiv needles
0.368 (var 0.111). arXiv haystack — arXiv needles 0.654 (var 0.0858), PG needles
0.394 (var 0.105).

**4. Haystack structure.** "Surprisingly, we find that structural coherence
consistently hurts model performance." "Across all 18 models and needle-haystack
configurations, we observe a consistent pattern that models perform better on
shuffled haystacks than on logically structured ones." The report flags this as
an interpretability question, out of its own scope.

**5. LongMemEval.** Used LongMemEval_s, filtered to knowledge-update, temporal
reasoning, and multi-session categories; 38 prompts removed as ambiguous or
unanswerable, leaving 306 prompts averaging ~113k tokens. Focused prompts average
~300 tokens. Judge: GPT-4.1 with ">99% alignment to human judgment."

"Across all models, we see significantly higher performance on focused prompts
compared to full prompts." The Claude family shows "the most pronounced gap,"
driven by abstention under ambiguity. Thinking modes improve both conditions but
"we still see a performance gap between the two input lengths even with full
reasoning capabilities on the latest models."

Framing of why: the full-input condition forces the model to perform retrieval
AND reasoning in one call, where the focused condition requires reasoning only.

**6. Repeated words.** A deliberately trivial replication task ("Simply replicate
the following text, output the exact same text: apple apple ... apples ...").
1090 variations per word combination across word counts 25–10000. "Even for these
straightforward tasks, model performance becomes non-uniform as context length,
encompassing both input and output lengths, grows." Rationale: the models are
autoregressive, so generated output re-enters the input.

## Conclusion (verbatim)

"Through our experiments, we demonstrate that LLMs do not maintain consistent
performance across input lengths. Even on tasks as simple as non-lexical
retrieval or text replication, we see increasing non-uniformity in performance as
input length grows.

Our results highlight the need for more rigorous long-context evaluation beyond
current benchmarks, as well as the importance of context engineering. Whether
relevant information is present in a model's context is not all that matters;
what matters more is how that information is presented. We demonstrate that even
the most capable models are sensitive to this, making effective context
engineering essential for reliable performance."
